# Round Table — Problems This Solves

---

## The 50 Problems Round Table Was Built to Fix

### Category 1: Group Fragmentation

**Problem 1 — Too many apps to manage one group.**
A typical family uses iMessage for chat, Google Calendar for events, Google Drive for files, FaceTime for calls, and email for longer messages. That's five apps for one group of people. Round Table puts chat, calendar, files, calls, and messaging in one window.

**Problem 2 — Different groups live in different tools.**
Your family is on WhatsApp, your church group on GroupMe, your project team on Slack. You context-switch between completely different interfaces all day. Round Table gives every group the same unified interface — just a different table.

**Problem 3 — No single place to see "what's happening across all my groups."**
You check Slack for work updates, then WhatsApp for family, then a church app for events. Round Table's Portal dashboard shows today's events, recent shares, unread messages, and live tables — across every group — in one view.

**Problem 4 — Starting a new group requires picking a platform.**
Want to start a neighborhood watch? You have to choose: WhatsApp group? Facebook group? Slack workspace? GroupMe? With Round Table, you click "New Table," name it, pick a color, add members. Done.

**Problem 5 — Group members use different ecosystems.**
Mom uses Apple, Dad uses Google, the kids use Microsoft for school. Round Table includes launchers for all three ecosystems (Apple, Google, Microsoft) — 24 apps accessible from one place, no favoritism.

---

### Category 2: Communication Overload

**Problem 6 — Group chats are noisy and unstructured.**
A 20-person group chat buries important messages under "lol" and emoji reactions. Round Table separates messaging (1:1 conversations) from shared items (on the table) from events (on the calendar). Each has its own space.

**Problem 7 — No quick way to "just talk" without scheduling a call.**
You want to ask your spouse a quick question. FaceTime means calling, waiting for answer, starting a full video call. Round Table's walkie-talkie lets you hold a button and talk instantly — like a two-way radio. No call setup, no ring, no waiting.

**Problem 8 — Notifications from every app compete for attention.**
Slack pings. WhatsApp pings. Email pings. Calendar pings. Each from a different app with different sounds. Round Table consolidates all notifications — walkie pings, shares, events, messages — into one stream with one notification bell.

**Problem 9 — You miss messages because they're in the wrong app.**
Someone shared an important document in the wrong thread, or sent it via email when you expected a text. Round Table puts everything — texts, emails, shares — visible from the Portal and Messages sections.

**Problem 10 — "Can everyone hear me?" — the video call setup tax.**
Every video call starts with 3 minutes of "Can you hear me? Is my camera on? Let me share my screen." Round Table's video call UI is immediate: one click, full screen, clear controls. The walkie-talkie mode avoids video call overhead entirely for quick voice exchanges.

### Category 3: Shared Content Chaos

**Problem 11 — "Where did that photo go?"**
Someone shared a vacation photo. Was it in the chat? In the shared album? In an email? On Google Drive? In Round Table, shared photos appear on the table surface. Click the table, see everything that's been shared, when, and by whom.

**Problem 12 — Files are scattered across multiple cloud storage services.**
The family budget is on Google Drive. The kids' school forms are on iCloud. The tax documents are on OneDrive. Round Table puts all shared files visually on the table with links to open them in any suite (Apple, Google, Microsoft) via the App Launcher.

**Problem 13 — No visual way to see what a group has been working on.**
Open a Slack channel and you see a text stream. Open a Google Drive folder and you see a file list. Open Round Table and you see a literal table with icons — a photo here, a document there, an audio note there — giving you spatial awareness of the group's shared content.

**Problem 14 — Sharing a file means knowing which tool to use.**
Do you upload to Drive, attach to email, or drop in chat? Round Table has one action: "Share Item." Drag and drop, or click a quick-share button. The item appears on the table for everyone. One action, one place.

**Problem 15 — You can't see who shared what, when.**
In a shared Google Drive folder, finding who uploaded a file and when requires clicking through properties. In Round Table, every item on the table and in the shared items list shows "Shared by [Name] · [time ago]" immediately.

**Problem 16 — Group editing requires everyone to find the same document.**
"Can everyone open the proposal? It's in the shared folder. No, the other shared folder." In Round Table, the document is literally visible on the table surface. Click it to open. Click "Edit" to collaborate.

### Category 4: Calendar & Scheduling

**Problem 17 — Family events are on one calendar, work on another, church on a third.**
You maintain separate calendars for separate groups, then manually cross-reference conflicts. Round Table's shared calendar shows events from all your tables in one view, color-coded by group.

**Problem 18 — Adding a group event requires knowing everyone's calendar app.**
"I'll add it to Google Calendar." "But I use Apple Calendar." Round Table's calendar is built in — click a day, add an event, pick which table it belongs to. Everyone on that table sees it.

**Problem 19 — You can't see events filtered by group.**
Google Calendar lets you filter by calendar, but you need one calendar per group and maintain them manually. Round Table auto-creates color-coded events per table, with filter chips to show/hide groups instantly.

**Problem 20 — Adding an event takes too many steps.**
Open Calendar app. Click new event. Fill in 8 fields. Pick a calendar. Save. In Round Table, click a day, type a title, pick a time, select a table, done. Four fields, one modal, one click.

### Category 5: Family-Specific Problems

**Problem 21 — No collaboration tool is designed for families.**
Slack is for companies. Asana is for project managers. Notion is for knowledge workers. None of them feel right for "Mom, Dad, and the kids managing soccer practice, grocery lists, and vacation plans." Round Table's table metaphor is family-natural. You sit at a table. You share things. That's it.

**Problem 22 — Kids' school documents get lost.**
Permission slips, report cards, homework assignments — they come in as PDFs, photos of papers, emails from teachers. Share them to the Family Circle table. They're visible, organized by who shared them and when.

**Problem 23 — Family shared photos require a separate app.**
iCloud Shared Albums, Google Photos shared albums, WhatsApp media gallery — each is a separate system. In Round Table, photos are shared items on the table, alongside documents, audio, and everything else. One place.

**Problem 24 — "Who's picking up the kids today?"**
This question gets asked in text threads that scroll by. In Round Table, it's an event on the shared calendar: "Pick up kids — 3:15 PM — Family Circle." Or a quick walkie-talkie ping to confirm.

**Problem 25 — The family budget spreadsheet is hard to find.**
"It's in Google Sheets... somewhere." In Round Table, Budget 2026.xlsx sits on the Family Circle table surface. Click to open. Click the Numbers/Excel/Sheets icon in the App Launcher to edit in your preferred suite.

### Category 6: Faith & Community Group Problems

**Problem 26 — Church group communication is scattered.**
Announcements go on Facebook. Bible study notes go in email. Prayer requests go in a group text. The sermon link is on the church website. Round Table puts all of it on one table — Study Notes, Group Photo, Sermon Video link, Hymn audio file — all visible, all organized.

**Problem 27 — Sharing sermon links or study materials requires multiple steps.**
Copy the URL from the church website, open your group chat, paste it, add context. In Round Table, click "Share Item," choose "Link," paste the URL, done. It appears on the Faith Group table for everyone.

**Problem 28 — Prayer groups have no dedicated tool.**
You can't really do a prayer circle on Slack. Group texts feel inadequate. Round Table's walkie-talkie mode lets a faith group do real-time voice prayer together — hold the button, speak your prayer, release. The beep notification lets everyone know when someone is speaking.

**Problem 29 — Small groups struggle to coordinate meeting times.**
Doodle polls, email chains, group text arguments about schedules. Round Table's shared calendar per table means the group leader adds "Bible Study — Wednesday 7 PM" once, and everyone sees it.

**Problem 30 — New group members don't know what's been shared.**
A new member joins the church study group and has to ask "Where are the notes? What did I miss?" In Round Table, they join the table and see all shared items — documents, links, audio — organized by time. Immediate context.

### Category 7: Project & Work Team Problems

**Problem 31 — Project status requires checking multiple tools.**
Task status in Asana. Latest files in Dropbox. Discussion in Slack. Meeting notes in Notion. Round Table shows the project table with Demo.mp4, Proposal.docx, and the team seated around it. One glance = project state.

**Problem 32 — "Is anyone working on this right now?"**
You open a shared document and wonder if anyone else is active. Round Table's Live/Dormant indicators tell you instantly: is the table active? Who's currently online? Green glowing ring = people are here. Gray dashed border = no one's around.

**Problem 33 — Quick team questions require opening a full chat app.**
"Hey, are you available?" — this message in Slack starts a thread, requires the person to open Slack, type a response. Round Table's walkie-talkie sends a beep ping. The person taps Answer. You talk. 5 seconds total.

**Problem 34 — Team meetings require a separate tool.**
Open Zoom/Meet/Teams, generate a link, share the link, wait for everyone to join. Round Table has video calling built in — click a member's seat, click the video button. Call starts.

**Problem 35 — Demo day files are hard to collect in one place.**
The demo video is in one person's Drive. The slide deck is in another's. The proposal is in email. In Round Table, everyone shares their files to the Project Alpha table. Demo.mp4, Proposal.docx, and the presentation all sit on the table together.

### Category 8: User Experience Problems

**Problem 36 — Collaboration tools look like software, not a shared space.**
Slack feels like IRC with a skin. Teams feels like Outlook's younger sibling. Round Table feels like sitting at a table with people you know. The visual metaphor makes technology invisible.

**Problem 37 — Learning new collaboration tools takes days.**
Notion's learning curve is weeks. Slack's is days. Round Table's is minutes: here's your table, here are your people, here's what they've shared. Click things. Share things. Talk to people.

**Problem 38 — Non-technical family members can't use complex tools.**
Grandma isn't going to learn Slack. Round Table has a macOS-style interface that feels like a familiar computer desktop — sidebar, dock, windows, buttons. If you can use a Mac, you can use Round Table.

**Problem 39 — Most tools are mobile-first, desktop-second.**
WhatsApp and GroupMe are phone apps. Their desktop versions are afterthoughts. Round Table is designed desktop-first with a macOS aesthetic — but includes responsive design that hides the sidebar and shrinks the dock on mobile.

**Problem 40 — Ugly, cluttered interfaces create friction.**
Enterprise tools cram every feature into every screen. Round Table uses white space, subtle shadows, smooth animations, and a carefully curated color palette. It's a pleasure to look at, so people actually want to use it.

### Category 9: Notification & Awareness Problems

**Problem 41 — You don't know when your group is active.**
Is anyone in the family group right now? Is the project team online? You have to open each app and check. Round Table's sidebar shows Live (pulsing green dot, "2 on") or Dormant (faded) for every table at a glance.

**Problem 42 — Important notifications get buried.**
An urgent walkie-talkie ping from your spouse gets the same treatment as a marketing email. Round Table's ping notification is a visually distinct toast with the person's avatar, a green Answer button, and a red Dismiss button — it demands attention.

**Problem 43 — You can't see what happened while you were away.**
Come back to 47 unread Slack messages and you don't know what matters. Round Table's Portal dashboard highlights: 2 unread messages, 3 new items shared, 1 new event added. Structured, prioritized, scannable.

**Problem 44 — No beep/sound for urgent communication.**
Text messages are silent unless you configure them. Slack's notification sound is the same for everything. Round Table's walkie-talkie has a distinctive two-tone beep (880Hz → 1100Hz) that is immediately recognizable — like a real walkie-talkie roger beep.

### Category 10: Integration & Access Problems

**Problem 45 — Apple users, Google users, and Microsoft users can't agree on tools.**
"Use Pages." "No, use Docs." "No, use Word." Round Table's App Launcher has all three suites side by side — 24 apps, filterable by platform. Use whatever you want. The file lives on the shared table regardless.

**Problem 46 — Opening a productivity app requires remembering its URL.**
docs.google.com? sheets.google.com? office.live.com/start/Word.aspx? Round Table's App Launcher shows all apps as visual icons with one-click access. No URLs to memorize.

**Problem 47 — There's no unified launcher for cross-platform apps.**
macOS Launchpad shows your local apps. Chrome's new tab shows Google apps. Neither shows the other. Round Table's launcher shows Apple, Google, and Microsoft apps together, treating all three ecosystems equally.

**Problem 48 — Switching between communication modes requires switching apps.**
Text in iMessage. Call on FaceTime. Share on Drive. Meet on Zoom. In Round Table, from a single member interaction you can: message them, walkie-talkie them, video call them, or see what they've shared — without switching apps.

**Problem 49 — Group tools don't show you the people.**
Slack shows channels. Drive shows folders. Calendar shows time slots. Round Table shows **people** — their avatars, their initials, their colors, their online status — seated around a table. It's a people-first interface.

**Problem 50 — There's no product that treats "a group of people sharing life" as a first-class concept.**
Every existing tool treats groups as secondary to some other function (messaging, file storage, task management, video calling). Round Table makes the group itself — the table — the primary object. Everything else orbits around it.

## Summary

Round Table solves **one fundamental problem**: the tools we use to collaborate with the people who matter most were never designed for those people. They were designed for corporations, then awkwardly adapted for families, friends, and communities.

Round Table starts from the other direction: **what would a collaboration tool look like if it was built for a family sitting at their kitchen table?** The answer is a literal table — visual, intuitive, shared, alive when people are there, quiet when they're not.

Every feature flows from that metaphor:
- **See your group** → Members seated around the table
- **Share with your group** → Drop items on the table
- **Talk to your group** → Walkie-talkie and video calls
- **Plan with your group** → Shared calendar
- **Message your group** → Built-in chat
- **Work with your group** → App launcher for every platform
- **Know what's happening** → Live/Dormant status + Portal dashboard

**One table. Every group. Every problem. One solution.**
